About

I am a first-year PhD student in Bioengineering at the Indian Institute of Science (IISc), Bangalore, working with Prof. Deepak Kumar Saini and Prof. Siddharth Jhunjhunwala. My research is on biological aging in the Indian population — why Indians age the way they do, and why the tools built to measure that are almost certainly wrong.

Before the PhD, I did my MTech at IISc on cellular senescence and cell state transitions. Before that, my undergraduate dissertation at Basaveshwar Engineering College, Bagalkote — serum metabolomics in glioma — was my first real taste of what it means to ask a biological question through data. Most of my early training was in mass spectrometry-based proteomics at IIT Bombay, which is where I published my first papers and developed the obsession with high-dimensional biological data that has defined everything since.

The real question underneath all of this is one I have been sitting with since long before starting the PhD: why do we have to age at all? BHARAT is chapter one of a much longer project.

Outside the lab

Classic rock is the one identity I consider fully my own — Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd, AC/DC. I have a background in theater and improv from my Master's years, which turns out to be surprisingly useful for science communication.

I read literary fiction and watch Iranian cinema when I need to remember that precision and beauty are not in opposition. Kiarostami proved that. I write essays and poetry when enough distance has formed from whatever I needed distance from. I have a Substack called The Rogue Transposon that I am working up the nerve to actually use — coming soon, I mean it this time.

I grew up in Bengaluru and Bagalkote. I live in Aryabhatta Hostel, Room 1129, IISc. The mess food is fine.
